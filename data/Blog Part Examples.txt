Why Is Pre-Surgery Knee Conditioning Necessary For Older Adults?
Pre-surgery knee conditioning is essential for older adults because it lowers the risk of complications during surgery and speeds up recovery time. It also prepares your body to handle the stress of surgical procedures, supporting a smoother recovery with less pain. Other than that, conditioning boosts blood circulation, reduces swelling, prevents blood clots, and improves joint mobility. To achieve these benefits, you can focus on simple, targeted exercises such as:
* Heel slides: Improve knee flexion and support better joint mobility.
* Ankle pumps: Promote circulation and prevent blood clots in the affected leg.
* Sitting knee flexion: Maintain the range of motion while keeping the feet flat on the floor.
* Short arc quadriceps: Use a rolled towel under the knee to build thigh muscle strength
* Thigh squeezes: Strengthen the thigh muscle and support the surgical leg during recovery.
* Single leg stance: Improve balance and core stability while engaging the affected leg.
* Hip abduction: Strengthen the outer thigh and hip muscles to support knee alignment.

What Tests Are Done Before Knee Replacement Surgery?
Before knee replacement surgery, surgeons conduct a Complete Blood Count (CBC) and a Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP). Your doctor may also perform a Coagulation Test and an electrocardiogram (EKG). These tests give your surgical team essential details about your overall health and how well your body can handle knee surgery. Here’s what each test involves and why it matters:
* Complete Blood Count (CBC): This test checks red/white blood cell levels and platelets. It also helps detect anemia, infection, or clotting issues that could affect healing or increase surgical risks.
* Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP): The BMP measures electrolytes, kidney function, and blood sugar levels. These results are essential because imbalances like low sodium/potassium, or poor kidney function can affect your anesthesia response.
* Coagulation Tests: These tests show how quickly your blood can clot. They help prevent excessive bleeding during surgery and reduce the risk of post-surgical complications.
* Electrocardiogram (EKG): An EKG records your heart’s electrical activity. It helps identify heart rhythm issues or underlying conditions that need attention before undergoing surgery.

How Do Doctors Decide When It’s Safe to Ski Again After ACL Surgery?
Doctors decide if it’s safe to ski after ACL surgery by using clinical tests, strength measurements, and functional assessments. They start with stability checks like the pivot shift test, single-leg hop tests, limb symmetry index, and KT-1000 arthrometer to confirm graft integrity. Here’s how each test helps doctors determine if your knee is strong enough for skiing:
Pivot Shift Test
In this test, the doctor gently bends and twists your knee to see if it moves out of place. If it stays in place, your ACL is stable and can handle twisting motions. This is important because skiing involves frequent turns and pivots, so passing this sport test helps doctors decide if your knee is ready for the slopes.
KT-1000 Arthrometer
In this assessment, the doctor gently pushes your shinbone forward using a small machine that measures exactly how much your knee moves. According to PubMed, doctors often compare this movement to your healthy knee. If the difference is 2–3 mm, it indicates your ACL is strong enough, and you can safely start skiing again.
Limb Symmetry Index (≥90%)
Doctors measure the strength of your injured leg using exercises like leg presses or knee extensions. Then, they compare the results to your healthy leg and calculate a percentage. If your injured leg reaches 90% of your healthy leg’s strength, it shows your knee is strong enough for skiing.
Single-Leg Hop Tests
In single-leg hop tests, you hop forward on your injured leg and try to land steadily. During this, doctors observe he distance you jump, your balance, and how stable your knee joint feels. If your hops are controlled, it shows your knee is ready for the sudden movements required in skiing.

What are the Risks of Skiing with an ACL or MCL Sprain?
If you ski with an ACL or MCL sprain, you risk worsening the existing injury or damaging other ligaments in your knee joint. You may also strain the meniscus, lose knee stability, and face a longer recovery time. Besides that, if you continue skiing with an MCL sprain, you could injure the posterior cruciate or lateral collateral ligament. You may also face additional risks such as:
* Increased risk of complete ligament rupture
* Persistent inflammation in the knee
* Increased chances of re-injury
* Higher risk of falls due to instability while skiing
Moreover, if you keep skiing with an ACL or MCL sprain, you risk developing post-traumatic osteoarthritis in the affected knee. According to PubMed, post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA) accounts for about 12% of all osteoarthritis cases. It often develops after joint injuries such as ligament sprains, dislocations, or fractures. These injuries damage the cartilage and trigger inflammation, leading to stiffness, ongoing pain, and long-term disability.
What Causes ACL and MCL Sprains During Skiing?
ACL and MCL sprains in skiing are often caused by a phantom foot or a boot-induced mechanism. These injuries can also result from valgus–external rotation force and hyperextension, which put sudden stress on the knee joint. Here’s how each of these mechanisms can cause MCL and ACL tears:
Phantom foot
Phantom foot happens when you fall backward and the tail of one ski gets stuck in the snow. This position forces the tibia to rotate outward while the body twists in the opposite direction. The unnatural stress strains the ACL and sometimes the MCL. According to a study on ResearchGate, the phantom foot mechanism causes 22.5% of all knee injuries.
Boot-induced mechanism
The boot-induced mechanism occurs when you fall and the back of your ski boot presses into your calf. This pushes your shin bone forward, stressing the primary ligaments (ACL and MCL). Since ski boots limit ankle movement, most of the force goes straight to your knee. This can cause partial or complete ACL tears and may also injure the meniscus.
Valgus–external rotation force
This injury happens when the inside edge of your ski digs into the snow while your knee bends inward and twists outward. The force puts heavy stress on the MCL, often causing a sprain or tear. The ACL can also overstretch, leading to combined ligament injuries. You may feel sudden pain in the knee joint and notice instability.
Hyperextension
When your knee bends backward too far while skiing, it can hyperextend. This hyperextension stretches the ACL and may cause a complete tear. Sometimes, the posterior cruciate ligament and nearby tissues also get damaged. In this case, you may feel sharp pain at the front of the knee, notice swelling, and experience difficulty keeping your knee stable.

How Do Doctors Diagnose Knee Sprains in Skiers?
To diagnose knee sprains in skiers, doctors often begin with the Lachman test, valgus stress test, and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). These tests help identify the severity of ACL and MCL injuries and examine for damage to other knee structures. For example, in the Lachman test, the doctor stabilizes your thigh bone and gently pulls your shin bone forward. By observing the knee during this physical examination, doctors can identify potential ACL tears based on its response, such as:
* If the knee feels stable and does not move forward, your ACL is healthy.
* When the knee moves a little too easily or feels soft, it may be a partial ACL tear.
* If your knee moves forward too much, it usually means a complete ACL tear.
Moreover, the valgus stress test checks the MCL by applying inward pressure to the outer side of your knee while the leg is slightly bent. If the inner side of the knee opens more than normal, it suggests an MCL sprain or tear. After these exams, doctors often conduct an MRI scan confirm the severity of the injury, detect any other injuries such as meniscus tears or damage to other ligaments, and provide detailed images of the knee joint.

What Causes Patellar Tendinopathy in Basketball Athletes?
Patellar tendinopathy in basketball athletes is caused by repeated stress on the patellar tendon during jumping and landing. These repeated movements overload the knee extensor mechanism and gradually cause small tears in the tendon tissue. When these tears fail to heal, they cause inflammation and degeneration in your knee, leading to ongoing patellar tendon pain. Other than that, the following factors can also cause patellar tendinopathy:
* Weak quadriceps and thigh muscles
* Reduced flexibility that increases tendon stiffness
* Poor load management during training
* Uneven playing surfaces
Moreover, according to the NIH, long training hours, poor landing strategies, and high jumps raise the risk of patellar tendinopathy. A higher body mass index (BMI) also directly increases the risk of patellar tendinitis. This happens because extra body weight puts more pressure on the patellar tendon, making the tendon tissue more vulnerable to injuries.
How Is Patellar Tendonitis Diagnosed In Basketball Players?
To diagnose patellar tendonitis in basketball players, doctors conduct physical exams, an MRI, and an ultrasound. After that, doctors may review your medical history to identify past knee injuries that can contribute to the tendon pain. During the physical exam, the doctor checks for swelling and presses the patellar tendon to detect tenderness. They may also ask you to straighten or bend the knee to see if these movements cause knee pain.
Moreover, MRI scans give the most detailed view of the tendon, detecting inflammation, degeneration, or partial tears. Magnetic resonance imaging also helps differentiate tendonitis from meniscus or cartilage injuries. Other than that, doctors might conduct an ultrasound, a quick, real-time imaging test that shows tendon damage, thickening, or new blood vessels. This helps confirm the diagnosis and allows doctors to track healing progress during recovery.

How Does Patellar Tendinopathy Differ From Runner’s Knee?
Patellar tendinopathy causes pain below the kneecap at the patellar tendon, usually from repeated jumping or overuse. On the other hand, runner’s knee (patellofemoral pain syndrome) causes pain around or behind the kneecap. This pain often happens due to cartilage irritation, misalignment, or long periods of running. Moreover, patellar tendinopathy worsens with jumping, sprinting, or climbing stairs. In contrast, runner’s knee pain increases with running, squatting, or sitting for a long time with bent knees.
According to Springer, patellar tendinopathy and runner’s knee also differ in their treatment approaches. For example, PT often requires targeted tendon healing through patellar taping, foot orthoses, or injections. However, runner’s knee treatment focuses on improving joint alignment with quadriceps and hip strengthening, correcting knee biomechanics.

What Diagnostic Tests Confirm the Severity Of A Knee Injury?
To diagnose knee injury, your healthcare provider may perform the Lachman test, McMurray test, or gait analysis. Here’s how each diagnostic test works in sports medicine:
* Lachman Test: This test checks for ACL injuries by gently pulling the lower leg forward while stabilizing the thigh. If the tibia moves more than the other leg, it signals a ligament injury or complete tear.
* McMurray Test: This test diagnoses meniscus tears by bending and straightening the injured knee. If you feel pain, hear popping, or experience locking, it indicates a meniscal tear.
* Gait Analysis: During gait analysis, doctors observe how you walk or run to detect misalignment, knee imbalance, or instability. They may use video recording, force plates, or motion sensors. These tools help pinpoint movement issues after a sports injury.
* X-ray: X-rays detect bone injuries like fractures, patella dislocation, or severe arthritis.
* MRI: MRI scans create detailed images of ligaments, cartilage, and soft tissue. They can confirm ACL/PCL injuries, meniscus tears, or patellar tendon problems that X-rays might not identify.
* Arthroscopy: Arthroscopy is a minimally invasive procedure where a small camera enters the knee joint. It lets doctors check and treat cartilage, ligament, or meniscus tears.
